Completion rate is the most commonly reported metric in L&D. It is also one of the least meaningful.

This is a piece I've been meaning to write for a while — not because the argument is controversial (most serious instructional designers already know this), but because the gap between what we know and what organizations actually measure remains stubbornly wide. Understanding why that gap exists is as important as understanding what we should measure instead.

## What Completion Actually Measures

When a learner clicks through every slide and passes the final knowledge check, the LMS marks the course complete. What that tells you:

- The learner had access to the course
- The learner's browser didn't crash
- The learner was present enough to click Next

What it doesn't tell you:

- Whether the learner understood anything
- Whether the learner's behavior changed on Monday morning
- Whether the training addressed the actual performance gap it was designed to close

Completion is a measure of access and compliance. Organizations report it because it's easy to capture, easy to report, and looks like evidence that something happened. It is not evidence that learning occurred.

## Why Organizations Keep Using It

The honest answer is that measuring what actually matters is harder and more expensive. Kirkpatrick Level 3 (behavior change) requires follow-up observation or supervisor assessment weeks after training. Level 4 (business results) requires connecting learning interventions to operational metrics — which means collaboration with business units that often have no interest in sharing their data with L&D.

Completion rate requires none of that. It lives in the LMS. It can be pulled in 30 seconds and put in a slide deck.

There's also an organizational incentive problem. L&D teams are often evaluated on training delivery volume, not outcomes. If your performance review is based on "courses completed this quarter," you will optimize for completions. This is not a failure of individual integrity — it's a systems problem.

## What We Should Measure Instead

The right metrics depend on what the training was designed to change. But here's a framework that actually connects learning to impact:

**Level 1 — Reaction**: Not just "did they like it?" but "do they think it will be useful?" Predictive satisfaction questions outperform generic ratings.

**Level 2 — Learning**: Knowledge checks are fine for declarative knowledge. For procedural skills, performance-based assessments that simulate the actual task environment are far more valid.

**Level 3 — Behavior**: The hardest to measure and the most important. Options include supervisor observation, mystery shopping, error rate tracking, or self-reported application surveys at 30/60/90 days post-training.

**Level 4 — Results**: Connect training to the business metric it was designed to move. This requires a pre/post design and control group if possible — but even directional data is more useful than completion percentages.

## Getting Organizational Buy-In

The measurement problem is ultimately a stakeholder problem. L&D teams rarely have unilateral control over what gets measured and reported. Getting to better metrics requires making the business case to people who currently find completion rate sufficient.

The argument that works: completion rate tells you whether employees were exposed to training. It doesn't tell you whether the training was worth the investment. If the organization spends $X on a compliance program, completion rate doesn't tell you whether that investment reduced risk. Behavior change data does.

Start small. Pick one high-stakes training where the business outcome is measurable. Design a Level 3 or 4 measurement approach. Report the results alongside completion data. Let the comparison speak for itself.
